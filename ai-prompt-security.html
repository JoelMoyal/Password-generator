AI Prompt Security: What You Should Never Share With an AI

AI assistants have become part of daily work: drafting emails, writing code, summarizing documents, generating passwords. They feel conversational and private, like thinking out loud. They aren't. What you type into an AI prompt may be stored, reviewed by employees, used to train future models, or exposed in a future data breach. This article covers exactly what to keep out of your prompts — and how to get the help you need without the risk.

What actually happens to your prompts

When you send a message to an AI assistant, it travels to a server operated by the AI company — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, or whichever provider the product uses. What happens next depends on the company's data policy, which is often long, ambiguous, and subject to change.

In practice, this means your prompts may be:

  • Stored for an indefinite period — to improve safety systems, investigate abuse, or train future models
  • Reviewed by human employees — most AI companies reserve the right to have staff read conversations for safety and quality purposes
  • Used for model training — unless you explicitly opt out, and even then, policies vary
  • Subject to legal disclosure — subpoenas, law enforcement requests, or regulatory inquiries can surface stored conversations
  • Exposed in a data breach — AI companies are high-value targets; breaches have already occurred at several providers

Important: Enterprise tiers and API access often have stronger privacy protections than consumer products. If you're using a free consumer product, assume your prompts are retained by default.

What you should never include in an AI prompt

  • Passwords & credentials Never paste a password, PIN, or passphrase into an AI chat — not to "check if it's strong", not to ask for variations, not as an example. If the conversation is stored, your credential is stored. Generate passwords in a browser-local tool instead.
  • API keys & tokens API keys, OAuth tokens, private keys, and secrets for any service. Developers frequently paste these accidentally when sharing error messages or code snippets. Treat any key as compromised the moment it leaves your machine — revoke it and rotate immediately.
  • Personal ID numbers Social security numbers, passport numbers, national ID numbers, driver's licence numbers. These are the core inputs for identity theft. There is no scenario where an AI needs this information to help you.
  • Financial data Credit card numbers, bank account details, IBAN codes, CVVs. Even partial numbers combined with other context can be dangerous if they appear in training data or a breach.
  • Medical records Health conditions, prescriptions, test results, therapy notes. Medical data carries legal protection in most jurisdictions for good reason — it is highly sensitive and frequently targeted.
  • Confidential business data Unreleased product plans, M&A details, client lists, internal financial figures, proprietary source code. Your employer's confidential information belongs to your employer, and pasting it into a third-party AI is often a direct policy violation — as several employees at major companies have discovered publicly.
  • Other people's data Personal details about others — colleagues, clients, patients — shared without their consent. Even if you anonymize names, contextual details can be re-identifying. Data protection laws in many countries apply to how you handle others' personal information, regardless of where you process it.

The specific risk with AI password generation

AI-powered password generators — including the AI Mode on this site — are useful for creating memorable, context-specific passwords. But they introduce a network request that local generators don't: your text description is sent to a remote server.

This creates a concrete rule: describe what you need the password for in generic terms, never include the actual password, account credentials, or personally identifying context.

Bad prompt: "Generate a new password for my Chase bank account — my current one is BlueSky2024! and my username is john.smith@gmail.com"

Good prompt: "Generate a strong, memorable password for a banking site — something I can type easily but wouldn't guess"

The good prompt gets you what you need. The bad prompt hands a third-party server your existing credentials, username, and institution. If you prefer to avoid the network request entirely, use the Random or Passphrase mode — both generate passwords entirely inside your browser with no data sent anywhere.

How to use AI safely

None of this means AI tools are unusable — they're genuinely powerful. It means using them with the same discretion you'd apply to any external service.

  • Anonymize before pasting. Replace real names, company names, account numbers, and identifying details with placeholders before sending text to an AI. "Client X" and "Company A" are fine; real names are not.
  • Read the privacy policy of the specific product you're using. Consumer chatbots and enterprise API access have very different terms. Know which one you're on.
  • Use local tools for sensitive operations. Password generation, encryption, and any processing of credentials should happen locally — in your browser or on your machine — not via a third-party AI.
  • Opt out of training data use where available. Most major AI providers offer this in account settings. It's not a guarantee, but it reduces the scope of retention.
  • Treat the AI like a smart colleague in a shared office. You'd ask that colleague to help you draft an email or debug code. You wouldn't read them your PIN number.

The bottom line

AI assistants are tools with access to servers you don't control, run by companies with privacy policies you probably haven't read. The information you send them is only as private as those policies guarantee — and those guarantees have limits.

Keep credentials, identity documents, financial data, and other people's personal information out of AI prompts. Use generic descriptions for context-specific requests. For operations that require real sensitive data — like generating a secure password — use a tool that runs entirely in your browser.
